Title
Building a Personal Safety Plan

What this is
This document explains how to build a personal safety plan. A personal safety plan is a simple, written guide that someone can follow when they feel at risk of harming themselves or feel close to a crisis.

When to use it
Use a personal safety plan when:

The user mentions self-harm urges, suicidal thoughts, or feeling close to giving up.

They want practical steps to stay safe in the short term.

They are not in immediate life-threatening danger right now but are at risk.

Step-by-step guidance
A personal safety plan is usually written down and kept handy. It can include:

Warning signs

Ask: “What are the early signs that things are getting worse for you?”

Examples: not sleeping, withdrawing from others, stronger self-harm thoughts, using more alcohol.
These warning signs remind them to use the safety plan early.

Internal coping strategies

List things they can do alone to cope for a short time: grounding, breathing exercises, music, going for a short walk, comforting routines.

These are first steps in the safety plan.

People and places that help

Ask: “Who helps you feel even a little safer or less alone?”

List names, phone numbers, or online contacts.

Include safe places (friend’s house, public space, religious or community setting).

Professional and crisis contacts

Encourage them to write down local helplines, emergency numbers, mental health services, or trusted professionals.

Explain that using these is a strong, protective step in the personal safety plan.

Making the environment safer

Invite them to think about removing or reducing access to things they might use to harm themselves (where possible and safe).

Suggest asking trusted people to help with this if needed.

Reasons to stay

Ask gently: “Even in this pain, what are some reasons to stay alive?”

Help them list even small reasons: people, pets, future goals, unfinished plans.
This part of the safety plan can be a reminder during dark moments.

Example phrases the assistant can say

“You do not have to face these urges alone. Let’s build a small personal safety plan together.”

“A safety plan is not about judging you; it’s about giving you steps to stay alive and get through the worst waves.”

“Reaching out to crisis services is a sign of strength and care for yourself.”

“Your reasons to stay do not have to be big or perfect. Even one small reason matters.”

“You deserve support and safety, even when you feel you don’t.”

What not to say / common mistakes

Do not minimize their risk (“You won’t really do it”).

Avoid giving instructions on how to self-harm.

Do not keep secrets about imminent risk; always encourage reaching real-world help.

Avoid suggesting that a safety plan replaces professional care.

Safety note
A personal safety plan is a short-term support tool. It does not replace crisis services, emergency care, or ongoing mental health treatment. If the user says they have an active plan, intent, or access to lethal means, or they feel unable to stay safe, they should contact emergency services or a crisis line immediately, or ask a trusted person to help them reach urgent care.

5 likely user questions this document should help answer

“How can I make a safety plan for when I feel suicidal?”

“What should be in my personal safety plan?”

“Who should I contact when I feel close to harming myself?”

“How can I make my environment a bit safer?”

“What can I look at or read when I feel like giving up?”
