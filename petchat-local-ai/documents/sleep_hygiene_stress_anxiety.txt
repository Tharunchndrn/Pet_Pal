Title
Sleep Hygiene for Stress and Anxiety

What this is
This document describes sleep hygiene for stress and anxiety. Sleep hygiene means simple habits and environment changes that support more restful sleep, especially when stress or anxiety makes it hard to switch off.

When to use it
Use this when:

The user struggles to fall asleep, stay asleep, or wakes up unrefreshed.

Stress or anxiety is making their mind race at night.

They want practical, non-medication ideas to support better sleep.

Step-by-step guidance

Create a wind-down routine

Encourage a regular pre-sleep routine for 30–60 minutes.

Examples: dim lights, warm shower, light stretching, reading something gentle.

Aim to do the same steps most nights so the body associates them with sleep.

Set a consistent schedule

Suggest going to bed and waking up at roughly the same time each day, including weekends, if possible.

Remind them that consistency helps the body clock adjust, which supports sleep hygiene for stress and anxiety.

Keep the bed for sleep and rest

If possible, use the bed only for sleep and intimacy, not for work, study, or long scrolling sessions.

If they cannot sleep after 20–30 minutes, suggest getting up, doing a quiet activity in dim light, then returning to bed when sleepy.

Manage light, noise, and temperature

Dark, cool, and quiet is usually best.

Suggest eye masks, earplugs, or a fan/white noise if needed.

Gentle strategies for a racing mind

Encourage writing down worries earlier in the evening, not in bed.

In bed, use grounding or breathing: slow breaths, or gently bring attention back to the present when thoughts drift.

Remind them that thoughts at 2am often sound harsher than in daylight.

Caffeine, screens, and heavy meals

Suggest reducing caffeine in the afternoon and evening.

Encourage limiting phone or laptop use in the hour before bed, or using night mode.

Avoid very heavy meals or large amounts of fluid right before sleep.

Example phrases the assistant can say

“Your body can learn that bedtime is a signal for rest. Small, repeated habits are key to sleep hygiene for stress and anxiety.”

“You don’t have to fix sleep overnight. Even one small change, like a 15-minute wind-down, is a start.”

“If your mind races in bed, it doesn’t mean you’re failing. It’s a sign your nervous system is on high alert.”

“We can’t force sleep, but we can create the best conditions for it to come more easily.”

“Being kind to yourself about sleep often helps more than blaming yourself.”

What not to say / common mistakes

Do not shame the user for their current sleep habits.

Avoid strict rules that ignore their life realities (shift work, caregiving, shared rooms).

Do not recommend alcohol as a sleep aid.

Avoid promising that sleep hygiene alone will solve severe insomnia.

Safety note
Sleep hygiene for stress and anxiety is a useful self-help approach but not a substitute for medical advice. If sleep problems are severe, long-lasting, or linked to other health issues (e.g., breathing pauses, nightmares due to trauma), encourage the user to see a healthcare professional. If lack of sleep worsens suicidal thoughts or makes them feel out of control, advise contacting emergency or crisis services immediately.
