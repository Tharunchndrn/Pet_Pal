Title
Building a Personal Safety Plan for Suicidal Thoughts

What this is
A personal safety plan is a short, practical guide that helps someone stay safe when suicidal thoughts start building. A personal safety plan is written before a crisis becomes intense, so the person can follow clear steps when thinking becomes overwhelming. A good personal safety plan is personal, realistic, and easy to use in the moment.

When to use it
Use a personal safety plan when someone has had suicidal thoughts, self-harm urges, or past crises and wants a clear plan for staying safe. A personal safety plan works best when it is created during a calmer moment, not in the middle of an intense suicidal crisis, because it is easier to think clearly and choose useful supports then.

Step-by-step guidance

Warning signs
Write down the early signs that tell you a crisis may be building. These can be thoughts, feelings, body sensations, situations, or behaviours. Examples: “I can’t do this anymore,” isolating from others, wanting to drink, feeling hopeless, not eating properly, or replaying painful thoughts.

Internal coping strategies
List a few things you can do by yourself to lower the intensity of the moment without contacting anyone yet. These might include slow breathing, grounding exercises, taking a shower, walking, listening to calming music, watching something familiar, holding ice, or sitting in a safe place.

People and places for distraction
Write down people you can spend time with or places you can go that help interrupt suicidal thoughts, even if you do not talk about suicide directly. Examples: a trusted friend, a sibling, a neighbour, a park, a café, a religious place, or another safe public space.

People you can ask for help
List the names and numbers of people you can contact and tell clearly, “I’m struggling and I need support right now.” Choose people who are likely to answer, stay calm, and help you stay safe.

Professional and crisis support
Add your therapist, doctor, local mental health service, nearby hospital, and crisis or emergency contacts. A personal safety plan should always include professional or crisis options for times when suicidal thoughts become intense or you feel unable to cope on your own.

Make the environment safer
Write down how to reduce access to anything you might use to harm yourself. This can include giving medication, sharp objects, ropes, or weapons to someone else, locking them away, or leaving a risky environment. Reducing access to lethal means is a key part of a personal safety plan.

Reasons to stay alive
Write down what matters to you enough to help you hold on, even in deep pain. This could be a child, parent, partner, pet, future goal, personal value, faith, unfinished dream, or simply the hope that things can feel different later. Even small reasons matter in a personal safety plan.

What not to say / common mistakes

Do not say “promise me you won’t do anything” as the main strategy. No-suicide contracts are not the same as a personal safety plan and are not considered a strong prevention tool on their own.

Do not create a vague plan like “call someone” without names and numbers.

Do not include coping steps the person dislikes, cannot access, or would never use.

Do not wait until the person is in a severe crisis to make the plan if you can help make one earlier.

Safety note
A personal safety plan is a support tool, not a replacement for emergency or medical care. If someone has a suicide plan, strong intent, access to lethal means, has already harmed themselves, or feels unable to stay safe, they need immediate real-world support from local emergency services, a crisis line, or urgent medical care. In Sri Lanka, you can also look up current suicide helplines and crisis support options through directories such as FindaHelpline for local contact details.
