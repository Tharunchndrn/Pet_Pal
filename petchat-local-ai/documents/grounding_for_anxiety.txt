Title
Grounding Techniques for Anxiety

What this is
This document explains simple grounding techniques for anxiety. Grounding techniques help you bring your attention back to the present moment when anxiety feels overwhelming. The goal is to calm your body, steady your thoughts, and help you feel a bit safer and more in control.

When to use it
Use grounding techniques for anxiety when:

Your thoughts are racing or stuck on “what if” worries.

You feel panicky, detached, or “not really here”.

Your body feels tense, shaky, or on edge.

You want a quick tool to use before or after a stressful event, or while waiting for other support.

Step-by-step guidance

Name the feeling
Silently say to yourself: “I notice anxiety is here.” Naming anxiety can reduce its power a little.

5–4–3–2–1 senses exercise

Look around and name 5 things you can see.

Notice 4 things you can feel (chair under you, feet on the floor, clothes on your skin).

Listen for 3 sounds you can hear.

Notice 2 things you can smell.

Notice 1 thing you can taste.
Move slowly through each sense. If you lose count, gently restart.

Grounding with touch

Press your feet firmly into the ground and notice the pressure.

Hold a solid object (a mug, keys, a stone) and describe its shape, temperature, and texture in your mind.

Grounding with breath

Breathe in through your nose for a slow count of 4.

Hold for 2.

Breathe out through your mouth for a slow count of 6.

Repeat for 1–3 minutes.
Focus on the feeling of air moving in and out.

Simple mental grounding

Name 5 colours you can see.

Count backwards from 20.

Recite a short poem, song lyric, or prayer you know by heart.

Example phrases the assistant can say

“Let’s try a grounding technique for anxiety together. Can you name five things you can see right now?”

“Notice your feet on the floor. Press them down gently and feel the support beneath you.”

“Your mind is racing; that’s what anxiety does. For the next minute, let’s only focus on your breathing.”

“Anxiety wants you to live in ‘what ifs’. Grounding brings you back to ‘right now’.”

“You’re not doing anything wrong. These grounding steps are small ways to help your nervous system settle.”

What not to say / common mistakes

Do not say “Just calm down” or “There’s nothing to be anxious about.” This feels invalidating.

Avoid long lectures or complex explanations when someone is very anxious.

Do not push a specific grounding technique if they say it feels uncomfortable or triggering. Offer alternatives.

Avoid promising that anxiety will “go away forever”. Focus on managing this moment.

Safety note
Grounding techniques for anxiety are coping tools, not a cure and not a replacement for therapy or medical care. If anxiety is frequent, severe, or stops the person from living their life, encourage them to speak with a mental health professional or doctor. If they ever feel at risk of harming themselves or others, or feel completely unable to cope, they should contact emergency or crisis services in their area immediately.
