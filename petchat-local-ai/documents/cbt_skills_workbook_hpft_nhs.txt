RAG Knowledge Document: CBT Skills for Low Mood, Anxiety, and Panic
Source: Hertfordshire Partnership University NHS Foundation Trust - CBT Skills Workbook (Adapted)

What This Is:
This document provides practical, evidence-based tools from Cognitive Behavioural Therapy (CBT) to help manage symptoms of low mood, depression, anxiety, worry, stress, and panic. CBT is a practical approach that helps you understand how your thoughts, physical feelings, emotions, and behaviours are all connected and can create a vicious cycle. The goal is to learn skills to break this cycle.

When to Use This Information:
Use this guide to find practical techniques for managing a difficult moment. You can use it to understand what you're experiencing, find a specific tool to try (like a grounding exercise or a thought-challenging worksheet), or to learn about a concept like the fight-or-flight response.

1. Understanding the Core Problem
When someone is struggling with low mood, anxiety, panic, or worry, their experience is often maintained by a vicious cycle involving four key areas:

Thoughts: Negative or catastrophic thoughts like "I'm a failure" (in depression) or "Something awful is going to happen" (in anxiety).

Physical Symptoms: Racing heart, tension, fatigue, poor sleep, or loss of appetite.

Behaviours: What you do or don't do, like avoiding situations, withdrawing from people, or using safety behaviours.

Emotions: Feelings like sadness, fear, nervousness, or irritability.

The goal of CBT is to break this cycle by changing unhelpful behaviours and challenging unhelpful thoughts.

Safety Note: If you are experiencing thoughts of harming yourself or others, or if you feel life is not worth living, please seek help immediately. Contact your GP for an urgent appointment, call 111, or go to your nearest A&E. You can also call the Samaritans at 116 123.

2. Tool: Grounding in the Present (For Worry & Anxiety)
When you're stuck in a spiral of worry or anxiety, your mind is focused on future threats. Grounding helps bring your attention back to the present moment.

When to use it: When you feel overwhelmed by worry, feel anxious, or notice your thoughts are racing about the future.

Steps:

Stop and Notice: Pause what you're doing.

Use Your 5 Senses: Find one thing you can:

See: Look around and find something you can see. Notice its colour, shape, and texture.

Hear: Listen carefully. What sounds can you hear? A clock ticking? A car outside? Your own breathing?

Feel: Notice a physical sensation. The feeling of your feet on the floor, the fabric of your clothes, a smooth stone in your hand.

Smell: Is there a smell you can identify? The air, a candle, a cup of tea.

Taste: Can you taste anything? A sip of water, the mint from your toothpaste.

Example phrases the assistant can say:

"It sounds like you're stuck in a worry loop. Let's try to bring you back to the present. Can you tell me three things you can see right now?"

"That feeling of being overwhelmed is a sign you're in the future, not the now. Let's ground ourselves. What's one thing you can hear?"

"Let's focus on the present for a moment. What can you feel? The ground under your feet? The chair beneath you?"

What not to say: "Just stop worrying." "It's all in your head."

3. Tool: The ABC Model (Understanding Your Cycle)
This model helps you map out the vicious cycle of a difficult situation.

When to use it: To understand why you reacted a certain way in a specific situation and to see how your thoughts, feelings, and behaviours are connected.

The ABCs:

A = Situation: What was happening? Where were you? Who were you with?

B = Thoughts: What went through your mind? (e.g., "They think I'm stupid," "I'm going to have a panic attack.")

C = Consequences: This includes your Emotions (e.g., anxious, sad, angry) and your Behaviours (e.g., leaving, avoiding, arguing), and Physical Symptoms (e.g., heart racing, tension).

Example: A person goes to a party (Situation). They think, "Everyone thinks I'm boring" (Thought). They feel anxious and lonely (Emotion). Their heart starts pounding (Physical). They leave early without saying goodbye (Behaviour).

How to use it:
Ask the user to describe a recent difficult situation. Gently guide them to identify the thought that came right before the strong emotion. This helps them see the link, which is the first step to changing it.

4. Tool: Behavioural Activation (For Low Mood)
Low mood leads to inactivity, which makes the mood worse. Behavioural Activation is about breaking that cycle by increasing activity, especially pleasurable and necessary tasks, even when you don't feel like it.

When to use it: When someone is feeling depressed, lethargic, and has withdrawn from activities.

The Principle: Follow the plan, not the mood. You wait for motivation to do something, you'll likely never do it. Instead, you schedule activities and do them, and the motivation often follows.

Steps:

List Activities: Write down three types of activities:

Routine: Getting dressed, brushing teeth, eating.

Pleasurable: A hobby, seeing a friend, listening to music.

Necessary: Paying a bill, doing a chore.

Create a Hierarchy: Rank the activities from easiest to hardest.

Schedule: Use a weekly planner to schedule when you'll do them. Start with the easiest ones.

Do It: Carry out the activity, regardless of how you feel. (e.g., "Even though I don't want to, I will go for a 10-minute walk at 10am.")

Review: After a week, look at what you did. How did it affect your mood? What was the most helpful?

Example phrases the assistant can say:

"It's common to lose motivation when you're low. But motivation often comes after we start. Let's pick one small, easy thing you could do today."

"What's one tiny thing you used to enjoy that you've stopped doing? Let's plan to do it for just 5 minutes, no pressure."

"Don't wait until you 'feel like it'. That feeling might not come. Let's just set a time to do it."

5. Tool: Graded Exposure (For Facing Fears & Anxiety)
Avoidance provides short-term relief but makes the fear stronger in the long run. Graded Exposure is a structured way to face fears gradually so your anxiety naturally reduces over time.

When to use it: When a user is avoiding specific situations, places, or objects due to fear or anxiety (e.g., social situations, driving, dogs).

The Four Rules:

Graded: Break your fear down into small steps, from least scary to most scary. This is your "anxiety ladder."

Prolonged: Stay in the situation long enough for your anxiety to reduce by at least half. Don't leave when it's at its peak. This is how you teach your brain the situation isn't dangerous.

Repeated: Practice each step multiple times (e.g., 4-5 times a week) before moving to the next one.

Without Distraction: Avoid using "safety behaviours" (like a phone, a friend, a water bottle) to distract yourself. You need to experience the anxiety and see it go down on its own.

How to build an Anxiety Ladder:

Step 1 (Least Scary): Imagine the situation.

Step 2: Look at a picture or video.

Step 3: Go to the location with a friend.

Step 4: Go to the location alone for a short time.

Step 5: Go to the location alone for a longer time.

6. Tool: Containing Worry (The "Worry Time" Technique)
This technique helps you regain control over excessive worry by postponing it to a specific, scheduled time.

When to use it: When a user's worry feels constant, uncontrollable, and is interfering with their daily life.

How to do it:

Set a "Worry Appointment": Choose a specific time and place for worrying (e.g., 6pm in the living room for 20 minutes). Do not do this in your bedroom or right before bed.

Write Down Worries: Throughout the day, when you notice a worry, write it down in a notebook or on paper. Tell yourself, "I will worry about this at my scheduled time."

Refocus: After writing it down, gently bring your attention back to what you were doing. Use the 5 senses grounding technique.

Worry at the Appointment: At your scheduled time, sit down and worry about the things on your list for the full 20 minutes. You can think them through, plan, or just let yourself ruminate.

Close the Session: When the time is up, stop. Close the notebook. The worry is done for the day.

Example phrases the assistant can say:

"This is a common worry that keeps popping up. Let's put it aside for now. We'll give it our full attention at 6pm tonight."

"It sounds like your brain is trying to solve a problem that can't be solved right now. Let's put that worry in a box and schedule time for it later."

"What's a good time you can set aside to focus just on worrying? The rest of the day, you can be free."

7. Tool: Problem Solving (For Practical Worries)
Sometimes a worry isn't hypothetical—it's a real, practical problem that needs a solution. This structured approach helps tackle it.

When to use it: When the worry is about a current, solvable problem (e.g., a bill to pay, a work conflict, a schedule conflict).

The 7 Steps:

Identify the Problem: Be specific. "I can't pick up my son and attend the interview at the same time."

List All Possible Solutions: Don't judge them yet. Just list options. (e.g., ask partner, ask friend, change interview time).

Evaluate Pros & Cons: For each solution, list the advantages and disadvantages.

Select a Solution: Pick the one that seems most workable.

Plan: Write down exactly what you need to do to make that solution happen.

Do It: Put the plan into action.

Review: How did it go? If it didn't work, go back to step 2 and try another solution.

8. Tool: Cognitive Restructuring (Challenging Negative Thoughts)
This is a core CBT skill for changing the negative thinking patterns that fuel low mood and anxiety.

When to use it: When a user is having a strong, unhelpful thought (e.g., "I'm a failure," "Everyone hates me") that they seem to believe is true.

Steps:

Catch the Thought: Identify the specific "Hot Thought" that caused the strong emotion. Rate how much you believe it (0-100%).

Look for Evidence: Act as a judge. What is the factual evidence that supports this thought? What is the factual evidence against it? (Not opinions, but facts).

Create a Balanced Thought: Write a new, more realistic thought that takes both sides of the evidence into account. It is not a "positive" thought, but a fair and balanced one. Re-rate your belief in this new thought and your emotion.

Example:

Hot Thought: "I'm no good. I'll never find a job." (Belief: 80%)

Evidence For: I didn't get the last job I applied for.

Evidence Against: I got an interview in the first place. I have gotten jobs in the past. The feedback said I had impressed them and gave me things to work on.

Balanced Thought: "I didn't get this one job, which is disappointing, but I have gotten jobs before, and the interview feedback gave me useful pointers for the next one." (Belief: 70%)

9. Lifestyle: Sleep Hygiene
Poor sleep can worsen both anxiety and low mood. Sleep hygiene is about creating habits that promote good sleep.

Key Tips:

Routine: Go to bed and get up at the same time every day, even on weekends.

The "15-Minute Rule": If you are not asleep in 15 minutes, get out of bed. Do something quiet and relaxing in another room until you feel sleepy, then go back to bed. This breaks the association between your bed and being awake.

Bed is for Sleep (and Sex): Don't watch TV, use your phone, or eat in bed.

Avoid Stimulants: Limit caffeine and nicotine, especially 4 hours before bed.

Create a Wind-Down Routine: A warm bath, reading, or listening to quiet music can signal to your body it's time to sleep.

10. Crisis & Safety Planning
It's crucial to have a plan for when things get worse.

Key Questions for a Safety Plan:

What are my triggers that things are getting worse? (e.g., withdrawing from people, sleeping all day).

What can I do on my own to help? (e.g., listen to music, take a walk, use a CBT tool).

Who can I contact for support? (e.g., a friend, family member).

Who can I contact for professional help? (e.g., GP, NHS 111, Samaritans: 116 123).

What is my local A&E?

11. Likely User Questions
"I'm feeling really anxious right now. What can I do?"

"I'm so tired and I don't want to do anything. How do I get motivated?"

"I keep having the same worry over and over again. How do I stop it?"

"What is a panic attack? Am I having one?"

"How do I challenge negative thoughts about myself?"
