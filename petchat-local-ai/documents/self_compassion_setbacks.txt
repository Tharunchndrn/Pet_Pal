Title
Self-Compassion During Setbacks

What this is
This document explains how to practice self-compassion during setbacks. A setback might be a relapse in symptoms, missing a goal, falling back into an old habit, or having a bad day after progress.

When to use it
Use self-compassion during setbacks when:

The user feels they have “ruined everything” after one bad day.

They are harshly criticising themselves for struggling.

They fear that a slip means they will “never get better.”

Step-by-step guidance

Normalise setbacks

Explain that setbacks are a normal part of healing and change, not proof of failure.

Many people take two steps forward, one step back.

Name the inner critic

Ask: “What is the harsh voice in your mind saying right now?”

Label it as an inner critic, not the ultimate truth.

Self-compassion break
Invite three short steps:

Mindfulness: “This is a moment of suffering.”

Common humanity: “Struggle is part of being human; I’m not alone.”

Kindness: “May I be gentle with myself right now.”
These phrases support self-compassion during setbacks.

Reframe the setback

Ask: “If this were a friend’s setback, how would you respond?”

Help them turn that response toward themselves: understanding, not attack.

Small next step

Encourage one tiny, caring action: drink water, rest, return to one helpful habit, or reach out to someone.

Emphasise that restarting is more important than being perfect.

Example phrases the assistant can say

“A setback does not delete your progress; it’s one part of a longer story.”

“You’re talking to yourself in a way you’d never speak to someone you care about.”

“Self-compassion during setbacks is not letting yourself off the hook; it’s giving yourself the support you need to try again.”

“You deserve patience and kindness, especially when things feel messy.”

“What would you say to a dear friend who was in your place right now?”

What not to say / common mistakes

Do not shame them for the setback.

Avoid messages like “You just have to be stronger” or “You shouldn’t feel this way.”

Do not demand that they jump straight back into full performance.

Avoid minimising real pain; self-compassion recognises suffering honestly.

Safety note
Self-compassion during setbacks is an emotional skill, not a replacement for professional care. If a setback includes self-harm, severe relapse, or suicidal thoughts, encourage them to seek professional help quickly and contact emergency or crisis services if there is immediate danger. The goal is to reduce shame so they feel more able to reach out.
