Title
Behavioral Activation for Low Mood

What this is
This document introduces behavioral activation for low mood. Behavioral activation is a CBT approach that helps people gradually increase meaningful activity to lift mood and break the cycle of withdrawal and depression.

When to use it
Use behavioral activation for low mood when:

The user feels down, tired, or unmotivated most days.

They have stopped doing activities they used to enjoy or find meaningful.

They want a practical, action-based way to support their mood.

Step-by-step guidance

Explain the cycle
Briefly describe: when we feel low, we often do less, avoid people, or stay in bed. Doing less can make us feel worse, which leads to doing even less. Behavioral activation for low mood aims to gently reverse this cycle.

List current activities
Ask them to describe a typical day. Note what they do and how they feel during each main block of time (morning, afternoon, evening).

Identify valued and pleasant activities
Invite them to list:

Things they used to enjoy (walking, music, hobbies, chatting with a friend).

Small tasks that give a sense of achievement (shower, cooking a meal, tidying a corner of a room).
These are candidates for behavioral activation.

Choose tiny, realistic steps

Encourage starting very small, especially in low mood.

Example: “Walk for 5 minutes outside” instead of “Go to the gym for an hour.”

Suggest 1–3 actions per day, not a full life overhaul.

Plan and schedule

Ask them to pick specific days and times.

Example: “Monday 10:00 – 5-minute walk; Tuesday 14:00 – phone call with friend.”
Writing a simple schedule makes behavioral activation for low mood more likely to happen.

Review and adjust

After a few days, ask which activities slightly lifted their mood or energy.

Adjust the plan to include more of those and remove what felt too hard.

Example phrases the assistant can say

“When mood drops, motivation often follows. Behavioral activation starts with action first, feelings later.”

“Let’s look for one small step you could take today that moves you toward feeling a bit more like yourself.”

“Even tiny activities count. A 3-minute walk or a shower is valid progress.”

“We’re not fixing everything at once. Behavioral activation for low mood is about gentle, steady steps.”

“You deserve moments of pleasure and achievement, even when your mood is low.”

What not to say / common mistakes

Do not blame the user for “not trying hard enough.”

Avoid pushing big, unrealistic changes that may lead to more disappointment.

Do not insist on social or physical activities that they clearly cannot manage right now.

Avoid making promises that behavioral activation will “cure” depression by itself.

Safety note
Behavioral activation for low mood is a helpful CBT strategy but does not replace professional assessment or treatment. If the person’s mood is very low, lasts for weeks, or comes with self-harm or suicidal thoughts, encourage them to seek professional help as soon as possible and to contact emergency or crisis services if they are at immediate risk.
