Title
What to Do During a Panic Episode

What this is
This document gives step-by-step guidance on what to do during a panic episode. A panic episode (or panic attack) is a sudden wave of intense fear or discomfort, often with physical symptoms like a racing heart, shortness of breath, or dizziness.

When to use it
Use this guidance when:

Someone says they are having a panic episode or panic attack.

They report sudden, intense physical fear with no clear danger.

They are afraid they are “going crazy”, losing control, or having a heart attack (and there is no sign of a medical emergency).

Step-by-step guidance

Validate and name it

“It sounds like you might be having a panic episode. Panic episodes feel frightening, but they are not dangerous in themselves.”
Naming a panic episode can reduce confusion and shame.

Orient to safety

Ask them where they are and whether there is any real danger.

If the environment is safe, gently remind them: “Right now, you are physically safe. Your body is reacting as if there is danger, but you are not in danger.”

Slow breathing

Ask them to place a hand on their belly if they can.

Guide: “Breathe in through your nose for 4, hold for 2, and breathe out slowly through your mouth for 6.”

Repeat for a few minutes. The focus is on the longer out-breath.

Grounding during panic

Use a simple grounding technique for panic:
“Tell me three things you can see, three things you can feel, and three things you can hear.”

Ask them to feel their feet on the floor or the chair under their body.

Normalize sensations

Explain briefly: “Panic episodes are a strong alarm system in the body. The racing heart, sweating, and shaking are uncomfortable but not harmful. They usually peak within about 10 minutes and then ease off.”

After the wave

Invite them to notice whether any symptom has reduced, even slightly.

Suggest a simple, kind activity after the panic episode (drink water, sit somewhere quieter, message a trusted person).

Example phrases the assistant can say

“You’re not alone; many people experience panic episodes. Let’s ride this wave together.”

“Your heart is racing because your body thinks you’re in danger. Let’s help your nervous system settle.”

“For the next minute, all you have to do is breathe slowly with me.”

“Notice your feet on the ground and the support beneath you. You are here, in this moment, and you are safe.”

“This panic episode will not last forever. We will take it one breath at a time.”

What not to say / common mistakes

Do not say “You’re being silly” or “It’s all in your head.”

Avoid arguing with their fear (“Of course you’re not dying”) in a harsh way. Instead, offer calm information.

Do not encourage them to escape every situation that triggers panic; that can make panic episodes stronger over time.

Do not make medical judgments. If symptoms sound unusual, sudden, or severe, advise urgent medical care.

Safety note
This guidance for panic episodes is supportive, but not medical advice and not a replacement for professional assessment. If chest pain, difficulty breathing, or other symptoms are new, severe, or different from usual panic episodes, encourage urgent medical help. If someone feels they might harm themselves during or after a panic episode, they should contact emergency or crisis services immediately.
